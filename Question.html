What I Question About Creativity

While much is known about creativity, there is a lot I still question. First, I question
whether creativity can be learned. We have learned that genes have been linked to creativity,
specifically a gene called neuregulin 1. Neuregulin 1 is present in many people with mental
illness and relatives of those with mental illness (Arehart-Trichel, 2012). This means that
creativity can be genetic, and in that way, certain individuals are more likely to be creative
than others. After learning this, I question whether creativity is mostly determined at birth,
or if it can be learned by individuals that do not have genes that predispose them to creativity.
I would like to see more studies on the impact of practicing creative thinking and if there are
anyways it can be learned and taught.

  I also question if it is possible to definitively recognize creativity. While inventions like the Swiffer are deemed highly creative, I wonder if it is possible
to say this is definitely so. While the Swiffer took imagination, divergent thinking, prior knowledge, and problem-solving to invent and resulted in a novel
product, I ask whether this involves creativity for certain or merely reflected the aspects to creative thinking. While it mirrored creativity, it could be
possible that the Swiffer was invented by seeing someone wipe the floor with paper towels and thinking to attach a stick. While the Swiffer and other
inventions very likely required creativity, I question whether we can ever know for sure.
