What I Believe About Creativity

While not proven, I believe that creativity is often enhanced by mental illness and
cannot be measured.
  While there is varying research on the topic, I believe that mental illness
can increase creativity. As The Neuropsychiatry of Creativity points out, there is a high
prevalence of mental illness in individuals who are widely considered creative. I believe
this is because mental illness can invoke a different way of thinking, which is needed in
creativity. This can be represented by having a different mindset, looking at the world
through a different lens, and having different perceptions of the same thing, seen through
people with bipolar disorder seeing colors as more colorful and smells as more intense
(Hermann et. al., 2015). When individuals think the same, they are likely to come across
similar solutions. On the other hand, having a different mindset and way of coming across
ideas can result in new and novel products. Although having mental disorders has not been
proven to increase creativity, researchers are on the path to this. Hermann and colleagues
reference a cross-sectional study that looked at features of mental health in comparison to
creativity. The study found that suprathreshold psychiatric symptoms and temperament features
play a major role in creativity (Hermann et. al., 2015). While this is not a direct correlation
to mental illness, individuals with mental illness display these traits.

  In addition, I believe that creativity cannot be measured. Some tests claim to do this, one of the most known and widely used being Guilford’s tests
on creativity. This tests would include questions like “name all the white edible things you can in 3 minutes”, “list all the uses you can for a brick in 3
minutes”, and “list the possible consequences of humans having 6 fingers in 5 minutes” (Weisberg, 2010). While this measures divergent thinking, like many
other tests for creativity it fails to measure all aspects that go into creativity. As mentioned, I define creativity as the use of imagination, background
knowledge, divergent thinking, problem-solving, and emotional expression to think of or create novel ideas and products. In this way, creativity tests do not
even scratch the surface of measuring everything that goes into creative thinking. Also, I believe these tests are useless because they use time limits to
measure creativity. Some of the most creative inventions have taken years to develop. For example, the Swiffer, a novel product took 3 years of observation
and creative thinking to invent (Lehrer, 2012). This does not mean the creators were not creative when developing the Swiffer but merely needed time to
gain background knowledge and a different perspective on cleaning to create this novel invention.
